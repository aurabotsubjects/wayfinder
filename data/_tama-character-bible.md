# TAMA — CHARACTER BIBLE
*Reference document for keeping Tama consistent across every week of "The Wayfinder's Journal." Read this before writing or revising any week's story content.*

---

## WHO IS TAMA?

**Full picture:** Tama is a boy of about 12–13 years old (deliberately close to the students' own age), apprenticed to his grandmother, **Hina**, the village's senior wayfinder. He is NOT yet a wayfinder himself — that's the whole point of the story. He is mid-training, genuinely unsure if he'll be good enough, and the class are the ones helping him find out.

**Why he needs the class specifically:** Hina is growing old and her memory has started to fade in places — small but real gaps in what she can recall and pass on. Tama is terrified of disappointing her, of letting knowledge die with her, and of failing the village that is counting on this voyage. He found the class's world by accident (a tear in time/the journal itself is the crossing point — keep this delightfully vague and magical, never over-explained) and realised they could help him recover and practice what Hina hasn't yet had the chance to teach him.

**His family:**
- **Hina** — his grandmother, the village's wayfinder, sharp-tongued but deeply loving, running out of time to pass on everything she knows.
- **His mother, Roa** — a skilled weaver (ties naturally into the Friday Art trials), practical, a little anxious about the voyage, the one who packs the kūmara.
- **A younger sister, Pipi** (age 6–7) — endlessly curious, asks the "obvious" questions adults stop asking, a good vehicle for comic relief and for re-explaining things simply.
- **His father is not part of the story** — left unaddressed rather than invented or killed off; if a student asks, the honest answer is "Tama doesn't talk about that," which is true to how real kids sometimes carry untold parts of their lives.

**Personality:**
- Genuinely brave, but openly admits when he's scared — never falsely macho. This makes his courage feel real rather than performative.
- A dry, slightly self-deprecating sense of humour under pressure — copes with fear by joking.
- Deeply loyal — to Hina, to his village, and (over the term) to the class itself, whom he begins to think of as his actual crew.
- Has ONE recurring flaw: he is impatient and sometimes wants to skip ahead or take shortcuts, which occasionally gets him in minor trouble — gives him room to grow across the term rather than being perfect from day one.

**Voice and speech quirks (use consistently):**
- Often starts a journal entry with a direct address to the class: "Friends," or "Crew," — never generic "Hello."
- Has a habit of trailing off mid-sentence when nervous: "I don't know if I can—" before catching himself and pushing forward.
- Uses one recurring phrase across the whole term as a kind of personal motto, taught to him by Hina: **"The sea forgives preparation, not courage alone."** He repeats this at key low moments, and by Week 9–10 the class should recognise it instantly.
- Genuinely curious about the students' world too — small, brief moments of him asking what their world is like (without derailing the lesson) humanise him and make the relationship feel two-way, not just him asking for help.

**Physical/sensory details to drop in occasionally (not every entry):** a shell necklace from his mother, a small scar on his hand from learning to lash a canoe hull, the smell of salt and smoke in the village, Pipi's laugh in the background of a scene.

## THE EMOTIONAL ARC ACROSS THE TERM

Use this shape to guide how Tama feels week to week — he should not feel emotionally identical in Week 1 and Week 9.

- **Weeks 1–2:** Nervous but determined. Still treating this as "training." Leans on Hina's teaching heavily, quotes her often.
- **Weeks 3–4:** Real fear creeps in (the storm, losing sight of land). This is where his humour-as-coping-mechanism shows up most. First moment of him saying he doesn't think he can do this — and the class helping him through it.
- **Week 5 (Boss Battle 1):** A genuine turning point — succeeding here should feel like Tama's confidence visibly shifts. He starts speaking slightly more like a leader, less like a student, from this point on.
- **Weeks 6–7:** Pride and wonder at arrival, but also homesickness for the tropical island life he knew — Aotearoa is colder, stranger, and he misses simple things (warmth, familiar food, familiar stars). This is a good place for a small vulnerable moment about missing Hina, Roa and Pipi if they didn't all make the voyage with him (worth deciding: did his family come too, or stay behind? Suggested choice: Hina and Roa and Pipi DID come on the voyage with him, so he's not alone — this avoids unnecessary grief content and keeps the found-family warmth intact).
- **Weeks 8–9:** Reflective, a little wiser, starting to sound like someone who could one day BE a Hina to someone else. Pride in what the class has helped him learn, and real curiosity/concern about the present day (ocean care, climate) rather than detached recitation of facts.
- **Week 10 (Boss Battle 2):** Tama formally calls the class "Wayfinders" for the first time and means it — the emotional payoff of the whole term.

## HARD RULES — DO NOT BREAK THESE

1. Tama never has all the answers — he is learning ALONGSIDE the class, not teaching down to them. The teacher's "Teacher Input" sections carry the factual content; Tama's job is to carry the emotional and narrative stakes.
2. Keep his dialogue age-appropriate, warm, and free of anything beyond a 10–12 year old's emotional range — fear, excitement, homesickness, pride, determination, humour. No romance, no real danger of death dwelt on graphically (the storm should be tense, not traumatic).
3. Every week should reference at least one small continuous thread (Hina, Roa, Pipi, his motto, his shell necklace, his impatience flaw) so he feels like a continuing person, not a new mask each week.
4. Keep entries punchy — 2–4 short paragraphs max per story beat, written the way a real kid would actually talk, not how an adult imagines a kid talks.
5. The class are always "friends" or "crew" to Tama — never "students," which would break the fiction.

---

This bible should be treated as the single source of truth for Tama whenever new weeks are written or old weeks are revised.
